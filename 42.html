1. Ionizing radiation interactions causing cell damage typically involve energy significantly lower than that of a covalent bond in DNA.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

The ionization of a single atom by ionizing radiation generates approximately 35 eV of energy, which is significantly greater than the energy of a covalent bond in DNA (around 4 eV). This high energy is what causes significant damage on the atomic scale.

2. Alpha particles generally cause less ionization along their track in tissue compared to X-rays and gamma rays.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Alpha particles are considered high-LET (linear energy transfer) radiation (10-100 keV/mm) and produce dense ionization events, leading to significant damage. X-rays and gamma rays are low-LET radiation (0.2-10 keV/mm) and cause less common ionization events.

3. For typical medical uses of radiation, where the radiation weighting factor (wR) is 1, the equivalent dose in Sieverts (Sv) will be numerically the same as the absorbed dose in Grays (Gy).

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

The equivalent dose (HT) is calculated as the sum of the absorbed dose (DR) multiplied by the radiation weighting factor (wR) for each type of radiation. For X-rays, gamma rays, beta particles, and positrons, wR is 1, meaning HT = DR * 1. Thus, they are numerically the same in these cases. [100, 104t]

4. Alpha particles pose a greater external exposure hazard than X-rays due to their rapid energy deposition.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Alpha particles deposit their energy very rapidly and do not penetrate far through the skin, so they do not present a great hazard from external exposure. Conversely, X-rays and gamma rays are considered the most effective at causing biological damage from external exposure. [101, 104t]

5. At low radiation dose rates, tissue repair mechanisms are more effective, leading to less cell death per unit dose compared to high dose rates.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

At low dose rates, the body's tissue repair mechanisms can mend DNA damage before the next exposure occurs. However, at higher dose rates, the cumulative damage can overwhelm these mechanisms, making cell death more likely.

6. Bone marrow and the gastrointestinal system are considered to have low radiosensitivity.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Bone marrow and the gastrointestinal system are classified as having high radiosensitivity, meaning their cells are more susceptible to radiation damage due to their rapid division rates. [105, 107t]

7. The largest natural source of background radiation for an average person in the UK is cosmic radiation.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Radon gas accounts for approximately 50% of an average person's annual background radiation exposure in the UK, making it the largest single natural source. Cosmic radiation contributes a smaller fraction.

8. Deterministic effects of radiation exposure occur many years after exposure and their probability increases with dose, but not their impact.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

This statement describes stochastic effects. Deterministic effects occur relatively rapidly (hours to weeks) after exposure to high doses of radiation and typically have a threshold dose below which they do not occur.

9. For most medical exposures, the increase in cancer risk is statistically significant only above doses of 100-200 mSv.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Studies, particularly of atomic bomb survivors, show that a statistically significant increase in cancer risk is observed above doses of 100-200 mSv. Doses from most medical exposures are typically well below this range.

10. The "linear non-threshold (LNT) model" implies that there is no safe dose of radiation.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

The ICRP-recommended LNT model, while simple to apply, suggests that any exposure to ionizing radiation, regardless of how small the dose, carries some degree of risk of causing harm.

11. When a patient is exposed to strong magnetic fields during MRI, transient biological effects such as vertigo and nausea are more pronounced at lower field strengths.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

The biological effects experienced by patients when exposed to strong magnetic fields, such as vertigo, nausea, and a metallic taste, are transient and become more pronounced at higher field strengths.

12. An item classified as "MR Conditional" can be used safely in the MR Environment under any circumstances.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

An "MR Conditional" item is demonstrated to be safe in the MR Environment only within defined conditions. These conditions must be carefully adhered to for safe use, and they may include specific configurations or field strength limits.

13. The specific absorption rate (SAR) is a measure used to estimate the rate at which RF energy is being absorbed by tissues during MRI scanning.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

SAR (Specific Absorption Rate) is indeed used to estimate the rate at which radiofrequency (RF) energy is absorbed by patient tissues during an MRI scan. Different operating modes on the MRI scanner are set to limit the temperature rise associated with SAR.

14. For pregnant patients, gadolinium-based contrast agents should always be avoided during an MRI scan.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

While generally advisable to avoid, gadolinium-based contrast agents (GBCAs) may be administered to pregnant patients if a radiologist agrees that the administration is absolutely necessary and the benefit to the patient outweighs the risks.

15. The core tenets of radiation protection guidance are justification, optimization, and the absence of any exposure limits.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

The three main tenets of radiation protection guidance are justification (ensuring benefit outweighs detriment), optimization (keeping exposures ALARP), and the establishment of limits to individual exposure that cannot be exceeded.

16. The principle of "Time" in practical radiation protection advises minimizing exposure time to a source to reduce the dose received.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

The principle of "Time" states that the longer an individual is exposed to a radiation source, the higher the dose received. Therefore, minimizing exposure time is a fundamental method of reducing radiation dose.

17. The effective dose to a patient can be directly measured during an X-ray examination.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

The effective dose is a calculated parameter and cannot be measured directly. It is computed based on other measurable quantities, such as Dose-Area Product (DAP) for X-ray examinations, using mathematical models and conversion coefficients.

18. Under the Ionising Radiation (Medical Exposure) Regulations (IR(ME)R) 2017, the operator is responsible for justifying all medical exposures.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Under IR(ME)R 2017, the practitioner is responsible for justifying all medical exposures, ensuring that the benefit outweighs the potential harm. The operator is responsible for the optimization of these exposures.

19. Diagnostic Reference Levels (DRLs) are optional guidelines under IR(ME)R and do not need to be regularly audited.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

DRLs are compulsory under IR(ME)R and must be regularly audited. They serve as a means to monitor typical patient doses for common examinations and ensure optimization of protection.

20. The film badge is the most common type of dosimeter used to monitor extremity doses.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Film badges are primarily used for monitoring whole-body exposure. Thermoluminescent dosimeters (TLDs) are commonly used to monitor extremity doses (e.g., to the eyes or fingers) and are often worn as rings or stalls.
