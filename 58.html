FRCR Part 3 Radiology Physics Quiz

Test your deep understanding of "Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd edition – Chapter 1" with these challenging True/False questions. Select an answer for instant feedback.

1. The number of electrons in an atom typically matches the number of protons to balance the charge, and this number uniquely defines the atomic number and position in the periodic table.

2. The binding energy of an electron to its shell is fixed for a given element and shell level, with L-shell electrons requiring more energy to remove than K-shell electrons.

3. When an inner shell electron is excited and returns to its original shell, the re-emitted excess energy as a photon is always consistent, yielding characteristic X-rays whose energy is unique to the specific element, but not dependent on the electron shells involved in the transition.

4. The valence shell, despite being the outermost electron shell, can hold up to 32 electrons to fully define an element's chemical and electrical properties.

5. All forms of electromagnetic (EM) radiation travel at the same velocity as light in a vacuum, and their photon energy is directly proportional to their wavelength.

6. Beam intensity, measured in W/mm², is typically easier to measure in reality compared to air kerma, which is an indirect measurement of energy fluence.

7. The inverse square law, when applied to a point source of electromagnetic radiation, assumes no absorption or scatter between the source and the point of measurement, and it indicates that the area of a beam is directly proportional to the distance from the source.

8. In X-ray production, approximately 99% of the electron's kinetic energy is converted into X-rays, with the remaining 1% generating heat, primarily through interactions with inner shell electrons.

9. High-frequency generators are used in modern X-ray equipment to maintain a near-constant waveform with minimal ripple (e.g., 1%), leading to advantages such as increased X-ray output and lower patient dose compared to older rectification types.

10. The K-shell binding energy for tungsten is approximately 59 keV, while the L-shell binding energy is 70 keV, and Ka X-rays are produced when an M-shell electron fills a K-shell vacancy.

11. Increasing the kilovoltage (kV) of an X-ray tube shifts the X-ray spectrum upwards and to the right, leading to both an increased total number of photons and increased maximum and effective energy.

12. Bremsstrahlung radiation accounts for approximately 80% of produced X-rays and yields a continuous spectrum of photon energies that is independent of tube voltage.

13. Filtration of the X-ray beam primarily removes higher-energy photons, which contributes significantly to patient dose but has little impact on image formation, and thus increases the overall output of X-rays.

14. The total filtration for a general radiology system is typically 2.5 mm aluminium equivalent, comprised solely of additional filtration sheets placed between the tube and the patient.

15. In X-ray or gamma ray interaction with matter, absorption is a stochastic process where the photon transfers all energy to the matter and disappears, making its outcome for any given photon impossible to predict precisely.

16. For a given material, the half-value layer (HVL) decreases as the atomic number of the material increases and as the incident photon energy decreases.

17. Photoelectric absorption is more probable at higher incident photon energies (proportional to E³) and in materials with lower atomic numbers (proportional to 1/Z³).

18. In most radiology applications, where photon energies are typically below 1 MeV, pair production is the predominant interaction, followed by Compton and photoelectric effects.

19. Charged particles, unlike photons which undergo exponential decay, have an ultimate depth within matter known as their range, which is dependent on their initial energy and particle type.

20. Fluorescence involves delayed photon emission facilitated by electron traps and intersystem crossing, while phosphorescence results in nearly instantaneous photon emission.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

1. Correct Answer: False. The number of protons uniquely defines the atomic number and position in the periodic table. While electrons usually match protons for charge balance, they do not define the atomic number itself.

2. Correct Answer: False. K-shell electrons have higher binding energy (EK) than L-shell electrons (EL), meaning more energy is required to remove K-shell electrons (EK > EL > EM).

3. Correct Answer: False. While the energy value is consistent for each element, characteristic photons' energy also depends on the specific electron shells involved in the transition (e.g., K-shell vacancy filled by L-shell or M-shell electron).

4. Correct Answer: False. The valence shell can only ever have eight electrons, adhering to the octet rule, even though an N-shell (if not the outermost) can typically hold up to 32 electrons.

5. Correct Answer: False. While all EM radiation travels at the speed of light in a vacuum, photon energy is inversely proportional to wavelength (E = hc/λ), not directly proportional.

6. Correct Answer: False. Beam intensity is difficult to measure in reality. Air kerma, on the other hand, provides an indirect but more practical measurement of energy fluence.

7. Correct Answer: False. The inverse square law assumes no absorption or scatter, but states that the area of a beam is proportional to the *square* of the distance from a point source, not directly proportional.

8. Correct Answer: False. Approximately 1% of the electron's kinetic energy is converted to X-rays, and the vast majority (99%) is converted to heat, primarily through multiple small interactions involving the *outer* electrons of target atoms.

9. Correct Answer: True. Modern X-ray equipment utilizes high-frequency generators to achieve a near-constant waveform with a 1% ripple, which indeed offers advantages like increased X-ray output and lower patient dose.

10. Correct Answer: False. For tungsten, the K-shell energy is 70 keV and the L-shell is 12 keV. Ka X-rays result from an L-shell electron filling a K-shell vacancy (Ka ≈ 59 keV).

11. Correct Answer: True. Increasing the kilovoltage (kV) indeed shifts the X-ray spectrum upwards and to the right, leading to both an increased total number of photons and increased maximum and effective energy.

12. Correct Answer: False. While Bremsstrahlung radiation accounts for approximately 80% of produced X-rays and yields a continuous spectrum, its photon energies are *dependent* on the tube voltage, not independent.

13. Correct Answer: False. Filtration removes *lower*-energy photons which primarily contribute to dose and have little image impact. Furthermore, increasing filtration *reduces* the overall output of X-rays.

14. Correct Answer: False. The total filtration for a general radiology system is the sum of both inherent filtration (e.g., from the tube housing) and additional filtration (sheets placed between the tube and patient).

15. Correct Answer: True. Absorption, like other photon interactions, is a stochastic process. This means that for any given photon, its outcome is governed by statistical laws of chance and is impossible to predict precisely.

16. Correct Answer: True. The half-value layer (HVL) decreases as the atomic number of the material increases and as the incident photon energy decreases, meaning the material attenuates the beam more effectively.

17. Correct Answer: False. The probability of photoelectric absorption *decreases* with increased incident photon energy (1/E³ relationship) and *increases* with the increased atomic number of the material (Z³ relationship).

18. Correct Answer: False. In most radiology applications (photon energies < 1 MeV), only photoelectric and Compton effects are considered. Pair production becomes predominant only at photon energies significantly above 1 MeV.

19. Correct Answer: True. Charged particles have a finite "range" or ultimate depth within matter, as their energy is lost through discrete interactions. This contrasts with photons, which undergo exponential decay.

20. Correct Answer: False. Fluorescence involves nearly instantaneous photon emission, while phosphorescence is characterized by *delayed* photon emission, often facilitated by electron traps and intersystem crossing.
