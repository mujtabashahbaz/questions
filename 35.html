MRI Physics Quiz: Chapter 6

Test your knowledge of Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) safety, signal principles, and image characteristics based on *Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd edition – Chapter 6*. Select True or False for each statement, and immediate feedback will be provided.

1. Most clinical MRI scanners primarily utilize permanent magnet designs for their strong magnetic fields.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False.
You were .
The sources state that most clinical MRI scanners use a **superconducting magnet design**, as permanent and resistive magnets are limited in the magnetic field strength they can achieve [6, p. 124].

2. During a quench, the liquid helium that turns into gas is not potentially dangerous as it does not displace oxygen.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False.
You were .
The source explicitly states that the gas produced during a quench **can displace oxygen, making it potentially dangerous** [6, p. 124].

3. The main magnet (B0) in an MRI scanner is always present and poses hazards even when the scanner is not actively scanning patients.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: True.
You were .
The magnetic field of superconducting magnets, once created, is **always present**, and thus the associated hazards are also always present [6, p. 124].

4. The MR Environment is defined as the volume surrounding the MRI scanner that includes both the 0.5 mT magnetic-field contour and the Faraday shielded volume.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: True.
You were .
The source defines the **MR Environment** precisely as the volume containing the 0.5 mT magnetic-field contour and the Faraday shielded volume [6, p. 125].

5. MR Safe items are typically made of materials that are electrically conductive, metallic, and magnetic, as they pose no known hazard.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False.
You were .
MR Safe items are specifically defined as being **electrically non-conductive, nonmetallic, and nonmagnetic** to pose no known hazard in the MR Environment [6, p. 125].

6. The primary nucleus utilized in clinical MRI is Carbon-13, selected for its large magnetic moment and widespread abundance in the human body.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False.
You were .
Most clinical MRI utilizes **hydrogen** nuclei (protons) due to their large magnetic moment and abundance in the body, not Carbon-13 [6, p. 129].

7. The Larmor frequency, which describes the precession of spins in a magnetic field, is inversely proportional to the applied magnetic field (B0).

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False.
You were .
The Larmor frequency is **directly proportional** to the applied magnetic field (B0), as shown by the equation u0 = gB0 [6, p. 129].

8. A 180° RF pulse is also known as an inversion pulse because it completely inverts the magnetization of the spins.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: True.
You were .
A 180° RF pulse is indeed referred to as an **inversion pulse** because it completely inverts the magnetisation [6, p. 130, Fig 6.11].

9. To achieve slice selection in MRI, a magnetic field gradient is applied perpendicular to the desired slice direction.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False.
You were .
In slice selection, the gradient is applied **in the slice direction** itself, causing the magnetic field to vary along that axis [6, p. 130].

10. Two-dimensional (2D) MRI scans typically offer good in-plane resolution but poor slice resolution, which means they cannot be accurately reconstructed into other planes.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: True.
You were .
2D scans are characterized by **poor slice resolution**, which limits their ability to be accurately reconstructed into different planes [6, p. 132].

11. In T1-weighted (T1w) images, tissues with a long T1 relaxation time, such as fat, typically recover signal very quickly and therefore appear bright.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False.
You were .
In T1-weighted images, **tissues with *short* T1 relaxation times (like fat) appear bright**, while tissues with longer T1 values (like CSF) appear darker [6, p. 133].

12. Gadolinium-based contrast agents (GBCAs) typically reduce T1 relaxation time in tissues where they accumulate, causing those areas to appear brighter on T1-weighted images.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: True.
You were .
Gadolinium is paramagnetic and, when accumulated in tissues, it **reduces the T1 relaxation time**, leading to a brighter appearance on T1-weighted images [6, p. 134].

13. The total scan time for a basic Spin Echo (SE) sequence is calculated by multiplying the Repetition Time (TR), the number of phase-encoding steps (NPE), and the number of averages (NSA).

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: True.
You were .
The scan time for a basic SE sequence is indeed **calculated by multiplying TR, NPE, and NSA**, indicating a direct proportionality [6, p. 134].

14. Gradient Echo (GRE) sequences are generally slower than Spin Echo (SE) sequences but are typically more robust to artefacts.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False.
You were .
GRE sequences are **typically *faster*** than SE sequences but are **more prone to artefacts** and usually have a lower signal-to-noise ratio [6, p. 135].

15. Fat saturation (FS) techniques are easier to implement at lower magnetic field strengths due to a smaller chemical shift difference between fat and water.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False.
You were .
Fat saturation is **easier at *higher* field strengths** due to the greater separation in the resonance frequencies (chemical shift) between fat and water [6, p. 135].

16. In Dixon imaging, adding the in-phase (SIP) and out-of-phase (SOP) signals effectively cancels out fat signals, resulting in an image primarily showing water signals.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: True.
You were .
By adding the in-phase and out-of-phase signals in Dixon imaging, the **fat signals cancel out**, effectively yielding a water-only image [6, p. 136].

17. In k-space, the central region primarily holds information related to image resolution, while the outer edges contain information about image contrast.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False.
You were .
The **center of k-space holds image *contrast* information**, and the **edges contain *resolution* information** [6, p. 138, Fig 6.23].

18. Patient movement during an MRI scan primarily causes ghosting and blurring of images, with the frequency-encoding direction being the most affected.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False.
You were .
Patient movement causes ghosting and blurring, but it **most significantly affects the *phase-encoding* direction**, not the frequency-encoding direction [6, p. 140].

19. In diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI), tissues exhibiting restricted diffusion appear dark on DWI images and bright on Apparent Diffusion Coefficient (ADC) maps.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False.
You were .
Tissues with restricted diffusion appear **bright on DWI images** and **dark on ADC maps** [6, p. 144, 144t].

20. Arterial Spin Labelling (ASL) is a non-contrast MRI technique known for its high signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), which typically allows for short acquisition times.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False.
You were .
ASL is a non-contrast technique, but it **suffers from *low* SNR**, which necessitates **longer acquisition times** to achieve acceptable image quality [6, p. 145].
