FRCR Part 1 Radiology Physics Quiz

Based on Chapter 4: Computed Tomography from Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd edition.

1. True/False: The scintillator in a solid-state CT detector directly converts incident X-ray photons into electrical signals, which are then processed for image formation.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. The scintillator converts incident X-ray photons into *light photons*. These light photons are then converted into electrical signals by a photodiode.

2. True/False: In CT imaging, increasing the field of view (FOV) while maintaining a fixed matrix size will result in improved spatial resolution.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. Pixel size is calculated as FOV / matrix size. Therefore, increasing the FOV while keeping the matrix size fixed will increase the pixel size, leading to *reduced* spatial resolution.

3. True/False: Streak artifacts from metal implants in CT are explicitly presented in Chapter 4 as being separate phenomena from those caused by photon starvation.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. Chapter 4 states that both high-attenuation objects (like metal implants) and photon starvation can result in streak artifacts, but it does not explicitly differentiate them as entirely separate mechanisms. High attenuation can contribute to photon starvation.

4. True/False: In helical CT scanning, a pitch value of less than 1 (e.g., 0.8) indicates a gap in data acquisition between successive rotations of the X-ray tube.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. A pitch value of less than 1 implies *overlap* in data acquisition. A pitch greater than 1 implies a gap.

5. True/False: When reconstructing CT images, a soft tissue kernel provides superior edge detection compared to a lung kernel, making it more suitable for visualizing fine lung pathology.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. Lung reconstruction kernels typically offer *increased edge detection* (though they are noisier) compared to soft tissue kernels, which are designed for broader detail. [75, 77f]

6. True/False: In CT, the patient dose is directly proportional to the peak kilovoltage (kVp).

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. Patient dose in CT is proportional to (kVp)Â², not directly proportional.

7. True/False: Ring artifacts in third-generation CT scanners are primarily caused by miscalibration or malfunction of a single detector element, leading to incorrect CT numbers along a circular path.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. A faulty detector in third-generation systems will result in incorrect CT numbers along the circle traced by that detector, producing a ring artifact. Daily calibration programs aim to prevent this.

8. True/False: The Straton tube, an advancement in CT X-ray tube technology, improved anode heat dissipation primarily by enhancing radiative heat loss within a vacuum.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. The Straton tube, developed by Siemens, improved heat dissipation by allowing *conductive* heat loss from the anode, which is a departure from the usual radiative cooling in a vacuum.

9. True/False: Multiplanar reformat (MPR) in modern CT scanners is limited to providing seamless viewing primarily in axial and sagittal planes, with reduced quality in coronal reconstructions due to inherent anisotropic voxel dimensions.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. A major advantage of modern CT scanners is MPR, which enables any acquired dataset to be viewed in *any plane* (axial, sagittal, coronal, or oblique), especially with isotropic datasets where resolution is uniform in all dimensions.

10. True/False: In CT images, quantum noise is primarily attributed to inherent electronic fluctuations within the data acquisition system, making it largely independent of the number of photons detected.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. Quantum noise is caused by a lack of sufficient photons and is directly affected by the photon flux (number of photons). Electronic noise, on the other hand, is inherent to the data acquisition system.

11. True/False: The collimator in a CT scanner is responsible for controlling the Z-axis beam width through its variable aperture sizes.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. The collimator is mounted on the X-ray tube and its variable aperture sizes directly control the Z-axis (longitudinal) beam width, also referred to as slice thickness.

12. True/False: A CT number of -1000 Hounsfield Units (HU) typically corresponds to air, representing near-complete X-ray attenuation in that region.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. While -1000 HU does correspond to air, air represents *minimal* X-ray attenuation, allowing almost all X-rays to pass through, not near-complete attenuation. High positive HU values (like bone) represent high attenuation. [77t]

13. True/False: In retrospective cardiac CT imaging with dose modulation, the tube current is intentionally reduced during systole to lower patient dose, as the primary data acquisition is focused on the diastolic phase.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. In retrospective cardiac CT with dose modulation, the tube current is reduced during cardiac phases outside the desired acquisition window (typically systole), thereby reducing overall patient dose while prioritizing high-quality data acquisition during diastole.

14. True/False: Dual-energy CT enhances image contrast by exploiting the differential responses of materials with varying atomic numbers to X-ray photons emitted at different energy levels (kVp).

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. Dual-energy CT leverages the energy-dependent attenuation properties of different elements (which relate to their atomic numbers) by acquiring scans at multiple kVp settings, allowing for material decomposition and improved contrast.

15. True/False: The CTDI_vol (Computed Tomography Dose Index - Volume) parameter in CT dosimetry is calculated by dividing the weighted CTDI (CTDI_w) by the scanner's pitch.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. The formula provided in the text is CTDI_vol = CTDI_w / pitch. This reflects the average absorbed dose within the scanned volume considering helical acquisition.

16. True/False: During the acquisition of a scout image (topogram) in CT, the X-ray tube continuously rotates around the patient to sum multiple 2D slices into a single projection.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. For a scout image, the X-ray tube is preset to a fixed position (e.g., 12 o'clock for frontal) and *does not rotate* while the patient table moves along the Z-axis.

17. True/False: An adaptive array in a multi-slice CT detector is characterized by detector elements of uniform size across the entire array, which simplifies reconstruction algorithms.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. An adaptive array features *thinner central detector sizes* that progressively widen away from the center. A linear array uses detectors of the same size. [72, 73f]

18. True/False: Dual-source CT scanners enhance temporal resolution by using two X-ray sources positioned 90 degrees apart, allowing for the detection of a half-scan with only a quarter rotation of the gantry.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. Dual-source CT utilizes two X-ray sources mounted at 90 degrees to each other, which, combined with a one-quarter rotation, effectively creates a half-scan acquisition, thus improving temporal resolution.

19. True/False: The "cupping" artifact in CT, resulting from beam hardening, appears as higher CT numbers in the center of the patient compared to the periphery.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. The "cupping" artifact is characterized by *lower* CT numbers in the center of the patient than they should be, due to beam hardening.

20. True/False: Increasing the pitch factor in helical CT scanning generally leads to an increase in patient dose due to the increased acquisition volume per rotation.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. Patient dose is *inversely proportional* to pitch. Doubling the pitch halves the dose. While it covers more volume per rotation, it reduces the X-ray exposure time for any given point.
