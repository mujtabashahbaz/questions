FRCR Part 1 Radiology Physics Quiz

Chapter 2: Radiation Hazards and Protection

1. A single ionisation event in tissue typically generates sufficient energy (around 35 eV) to directly break multiple covalent bonds in a DNA strand, given that a covalent bond requires approximately 4 eV to break.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: True. You were .

Explanation: A single ionisation event generates approximately 35 eV, which is significantly greater than the 4 eV required to break a covalent bond in DNA, meaning it can break multiple bonds.

2. Ionisation events caused by gamma or X-rays are comparatively as common as those caused by alpha particles in causing significant damage on the atomic scale, leading to a high likelihood of irreparable double-strand DNA breaks.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: Ionisation events from gamma or X-rays are rare compared to alpha particles. Alpha particles are likely to cause irreparable double-strand DNA breaks, while X-rays are more likely to damage only a single strand, which is more readily repaired.

3. At a constant total absorbed dose, a lower dose rate will result in more cell death per unit dose compared to a higher dose rate, due to the cumulative damage defeating tissue repair mechanisms.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: More cells are killed per unit dose at high dose rates because the cumulative damage defeats the tissue repair mechanisms before they can fully act. At lower dose rates, repair mechanisms have more time to operate.

4. Given their interactions with hydrogen atoms, organic polymers with a high density of hydrogen atoms, such as polythene, are effective shielding materials against neutrons, and these are commonly used medically.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: While organic polymers like polythene are effective shielding materials against neutrons due to their hydrogen content, neutrons themselves are *not* used medically.

5. Radon gas, an alpha-particle emitter, is responsible for approximately half of an average person's annual background radiation exposure in the UK, with its levels varying negligibly with local geology.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: Radon accounts for about 50% of an average person's annual background exposure in the UK, but its levels vary *widely* with location and local geology.

6. Deterministic effects of radiation exposure are characterized by a lack of a threshold dose, with their severity increasing with the dose received, which primarily leads to an increased probability of occurrence rather than immediate tissue damage.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: Deterministic effects *do* occur above a threshold dose, and their severity *increases* with dose. The description of increasing probability of occurrence with dose and not severity describes stochastic effects.

7. The International Commission on Radiological Protection (ICRP) recommends the linear non-threshold (LNT) model for calculating risk, which implies that there is no safe dose of radiation, a conclusion consistently supported by the data from atomic bomb survivors above 100-200 mSv.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: While the ICRP recommends the LNT model which implies no safe dose, this *contradicts* the evidence from atomic bomb data, which suggests a threshold dose (above 100-200 mSv) for a statistically significant increase in risk.

8. Effective dose is a directly measurable quantity that quantifies the total energy absorbed by the whole body from a radiation exposure, making it a precise indicator for individual patient risk with an accuracy of approximately +/- 5%.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: Effective dose can only be *computed*, not directly measured. It represents the uniform whole-body dose producing similar risk, but its accuracy for any individual patient exposure is around +/- 50% due to uncertainties in modeling.

9. According to the fundamental principles of radiation protection, dose limits set by regulatory bodies apply to patients undergoing medical exposures to ensure their protection and minimise their exposure to ionising radiation.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: Dose limits do *not* apply to patients undergoing medical exposures or to carers/comforters. Patient exposures are minimized through diagnostic reference levels (DRLs) and justification/optimisation principles.

10. The fundamental quantity of dosimetry in practical radiation protection, the absorbed dose to tissue (DT), is a calculated parameter measured in Sieverts (Sv), representing the biological effectiveness of radiation.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: The absorbed dose to tissue (DT) is a *measurable* quantity, measured in Grays (Gy). The *equivalent dose* is a calculated parameter measured in Sieverts (Sv), which includes a weighting factor for biological effectiveness.

11. In the UK, following the Medical and Dental Guidance Notes (MDGN) legally deems an employer compliant with the Ionising Radiation Regulations (IRR) 2017, similar to the Approved Code of Practice (ACOP).

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: Following the Approved Code of Practice (ACOP) legally deems compliance with IRR 2017. MDGN, however, describes good practice but does *not* carry the same legal weight regarding compliance.

12. An employer's responsibility to ensure the equivalent dose to a foetus is less than 1 mSv during pregnancy commences once they are advised of the pregnancy verbally by the employee.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: The pregnant employee has a duty to notify their employer *in writing* as soon as they have confirmed their pregnancy. The employer's responsibility formally records upon written notification.

13. Legally, an investigation must be carried out in the UK the first time a staff member receives a dose of greater than 15 mSv in a year, although most healthcare settings set this investigation level considerably higher to avoid unnecessary reviews.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: While an investigation is legally required at 15 mSv per year, most healthcare settings set their internal dose investigation levels *considerably below* this value.

14. An area must be designated as a controlled area if anyone working there is likely to receive an effective dose greater than 1 mSv per year, or if the dose rate exceeds 7.5 mSv per hour averaged over 1 minute and staff without radiation protection training are likely to enter, even if the source is a patient.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: An area is a controlled area if the likely effective dose is greater than *6 mSv* per year (not 1 mSv). The dose rate criterion of 7.5 mSv/hour (averaged over 1 minute for untrained staff) does *not* apply if the source of radiation is a patient.

15. The Ionising Radiation (Medical Exposure) Regulations (IR(ME)R) 2017 apply uniformly across all jurisdictions within the UK, covering only diagnostic and therapeutic medical exposures for patients.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: IR(ME)R 2017 does *not* apply uniformly throughout the UK (e.g., Northern Ireland has its own regulations). Furthermore, its scope extends beyond just diagnostic and therapeutic exposures to include health screening, medical research, carers/comforters, asymptomatic persons, and non-medical exposures using medical equipment.

16. The role of Medical Physics Expert (MPE) was clarified and formalised by IR(ME)R 2017, requiring MPEs to be formally certificated through a scheme administered by RPA 2000 in the UK.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: True. You were .

Explanation: IR(ME)R 2017 did indeed clarify and formalize the MPE role, making certification through RPA 2000 a requirement.

17. Local Diagnostic Reference Levels (DRLs) for CT examinations should be audited regularly and should aim to be consistent across different systems for the same examination at a given site, always remaining strictly below the National DRL (NDRL) published by Public Health England.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: While local DRLs are *generally* below NDRLs and should be consistent across systems, individual patient doses *may* exceed local or national DRLs, especially for larger patients or specific pathologies. The statement uses "always strictly below", which is incorrect.

18. Staff who are exposed to radiation while assisting a patient are categorised as 'carers and comforters' under IR(ME)R 2017, provided they do so knowingly and willingly, and their exposures are not subject to justification by a practitioner.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: Staff/employees cannot be categorised as carers or comforters for exposures occurring during their employment. Also, exposures to carers and comforters *must* be justified by an appropriate practitioner.

19. Thermoluminescent Dosimeters (TLDs) used for extremity dose measurement, such as finger rings, offer the best estimate of the dose to the tip of the finger without requiring correction factors, ensuring high accuracy to meet regulatory requirements.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: TLD *stalls* fit over the tip of the finger and provide the best estimate. Finger rings are worn at the base and *require* correction factors, and may not provide sufficient accuracy to meet regulatory requirements due to uncertainty in calculations.

20. The CT dose index-volume (CTDIvol) is a highly useful measure for comparing doses to individual patients, as it directly accounts for the total scan length and the number of times a particular region might be scanned, making it superior to Dose Length Product (DLP) for patient-specific dosimetry.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False. You were .

Explanation: CTDIvol is useful for comparing scan protocols and models, but is *less* useful for individual patients because it doesn't account for scan length or repeated scans. The Dose Length Product (DLP) is derived from CTDIvol and *does* account for scan length, making it more relevant for patient-specific dosimetry.
