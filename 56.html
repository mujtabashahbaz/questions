1. The heat rating of an X-ray tube increases with a longer exposure time.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False

The heat rating reflects the allowable milliamperage per particular kV values (kV * mA, with units in joules). This rating decreases based on factors like a longer exposure time, due to the increased heat production over time.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., p. 189]

2. In X-ray imaging, the intensity of the beam is most apparent to decrease from the anode edge towards the cathode edge of the field, due to the heel effect.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False

The intensity of the beam decreases across the field, and this is most apparent from the central ray (B) towards the anode edge (A). This means the intensity is lower at the anode end and higher at the cathode end. The statement implies the opposite directional decrease.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., p. 190]

3. An air gap between the patient and image detector plate reduces scatter and decreases image magnification, requiring less kV or mA to maintain photon flux.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False

While an air gap does reduce scatter, it produces a magnified image. Additionally, as the distance is increased, increased kV or mA is required to maintain photon flux, not less.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., p. 193]

4. For Computed Radiography (CR) systems, the photostimulable phosphor plate is commonly composed of barium fluorohalide doped with europium, and typically uses a red laser for scanning, as most phosphors release blue light.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True

The photostimulable phosphor plate indeed uses barium fluorohalide doped with europium (BaFX:Eu) as its active layer. A red laser is used to stimulate the release of trapped energy, which is re-emitted as blue light. This choice of colors optimizes the reading process.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., pp. 198-199]

5. Digital Radiography (DR) systems typically achieve a Detective Quantum Efficiency (DQE) lower than that of Computed Radiography (CR) systems.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False

DR systems have a significantly higher DQE compared to CR systems. CR systems achieve a DQE of approximately 30%, while DR systems can achieve around 65%. A higher DQE indicates better efficiency in photon detection and less added noise to the detected signal.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., p. 206]

6. In Digital Radiography (DR) direct conversion systems using amorphous selenium, electrons are released by the selenium and migrate to the charge collecting electrode with significant lateral diffusion.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False

In amorphous selenium direct conversion systems, the released electrons migrate to the surface electrode, and positive charge is drawn to the charge collecting electrode with minimal lateral diffusion. This minimal diffusion is crucial for maintaining high spatial resolution.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., p. 207]

7. A high-pass filtering technique in image processing enhances edges by increasing the weighting of pixels that contribute to those edges, and simultaneously reduces image noise.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False

High-pass filtering does enhance edges by exaggerating contrast at boundaries, but it also serves to increase noise. This is a common trade-off in image processing: sharpening often comes at the cost of increased noise.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., p. 220]

8. The Nyquist criterion states that the signal must be sampled at least twice in every cycle to accurately record high-frequency signals, otherwise aliasing may occur.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True

The Nyquist criterion is fundamental in digital imaging, stating that the sampling frequency must be at least twice the highest frequency present in the signal. Failure to meet this criterion leads to aliasing, where high-frequency signals are erroneously recorded as lower frequencies.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., p. 221]

9. For mammography, a Rhenium/Molybdenum target/filter combination is commonly used for larger or denser breasts due to its ability to attenuate higher-energy X-rays effectively.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False

A Rhenium/Molybdenum combination is not used in mammography. The text specifically states that the molybdenum filter would attenuate the rhodium-characteristic X-rays. Common combinations include Molybdenum/Molybdenum, Molybdenum/Rhodium, or Rhodium/Rhodium for larger/denser breasts.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., p. 241]

10. In an image intensifier, the photocathode converts X-ray photons to electrons with an efficiency of approximately 50%.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False

The photocathode, composed of antimony caesium (SbCs3), is only about 15% efficient in converting light photons (from the input phosphor) into electrons. While it produces 200 electrons per incident X-ray photon, its quantum efficiency is significantly lower than 50%.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., p. 247]

11. The brightness gain of an image intensifier is directly proportional to the minification gain and inversely proportional to the flux gain.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False

Brightness gain is calculated as the product of minification gain and flux gain (Brightness gain = Minification gain Ã— flux gain). Both factors contribute multiplicatively to the overall brightness intensification, not inversely.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., p. 253]

12. In fluoroscopy, the "anti-isowatt curve" for automatic brightness control (ABC) is designed to optimize the spectrum for imaging iodine, resulting in high image quality and high dose.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False

The "anti-isowatt curve" (Curve A) increases mA and kV as radiological thickness increases. However, Curve B is specifically optimized for iodine contrast imaging, holding kV at 60-65 kV and increasing mA, leading to high image quality and high dose.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., pp. 259-260]

13. To minimize patient dose in fluoroscopy, increasing the source-skin distance and using continuous fluoroscopy rather than pulsed fluoroscopy are recommended.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False

While increasing the source-skin distance does help minimize patient dose, pulsed fluoroscopy is recommended over continuous fluoroscopy to reduce dose rate. Pulsed fluoroscopy delivers X-rays in short bursts, reducing the total exposure time while maintaining diagnostic quality.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., p. 267 (Box 3.1)]

14. The `Radiation Dose Structured Report` in DICOM ensures that if an image is deleted, the record of the dose associated with that image is also lost permanently.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False

The Radiation Dose Structured Report (RDSR) was specifically implemented to prevent the loss of dose data when images are not stored or are deleted. Before RDSR, dose data was often directly attached to each image, meaning its deletion would result in the loss of dose records. RDSR ensures dose information is a separately exported DICOM image, attached to the main imaging encounter.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., p. 181]

15. In mammography, the compression paddle's function includes reducing the required radiation dose, scatter, and geometric and movement unsharpness.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True

The compression paddle is critical in mammography. Its primary functions include equalizing breast tissue thickness and, importantly, reducing the radiation dose required, minimizing scatter, and decreasing both geometric and movement unsharpness, thereby improving image quality.

[Source: Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd ed., p. 236]
