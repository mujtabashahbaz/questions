1. Ionising radiation is particularly hazardous to tissue because its interactions occur on the atomic scale.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: True
The source states that ionising radiation is particularly hazardous to tissue because its interactions occur on the atomic scale, enabling it to cause significant damage.

2. The energy generated by the ionisation of a single atom is roughly equivalent to the energy of a hydrogen bond in DNA.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
The ionisation of a single atom generates approximately 35 eV of energy, which is significantly greater than the 0.4 eV of a hydrogen bond in DNA.

3. Ionisation events, which can cause significant damage, are most common when X-rays interact with tissue.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
Ionisation events are common with alpha particles, less common with beta particles, and rare when gamma or X-rays interact with tissue.

4. In medical imaging applications, radiation doses typically encountered are on the order of Grays (Gy).

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
In medical imaging, doses are generally on the order of mGy or ÂµGy, while 1 Gy is a high dose typically found in radiotherapy for tissue destruction.

5. X-rays are classified as high-LET radiation, causing damage to both DNA strands.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
X-rays are low-LET radiation (0.2-10 keV/mm), typically damaging only a single DNA strand, which the body can more readily repair. High-LET radiation like alpha particles and neutrons cause double-strand damage.

6. Alpha particles have a radiation weighting factor (wR) of 1, similar to X-rays and gamma rays.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
Alpha particles have a wR of 20, whereas X-rays and gamma rays have a wR of 1, indicating alpha particles are significantly more effective at causing biological damage per unit of absorbed dose.

7. Alpha particles pose a significant external hazard due to their deep skin penetration.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
Alpha particles do not penetrate far through the skin, so they do not present a great external hazard. However, they can cause considerable internal damage if ingested or inhaled.

8. Tissue repair mechanisms are more effective at repairing DNA damage at high radiation dose rates.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
At low dose rates, tissue repair mechanisms can repair DNA damage effectively. However, at higher dose rates, cumulative damage can defeat these mechanisms, making cell death more likely.

9. Bone marrow is considered to have a low radiosensitivity.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
Bone marrow is listed as having high radiosensitivity due to its high rate of cell division. Tissues with slower cell division, like muscle or bone, have lower radiosensitivity.

10. The majority of the average person's annual background radiation exposure in the UK comes from medical exposures.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
Of the average 2.7 mSv per year from background radiation in the UK, approximately 2.3 mSv comes from natural sources (cosmic, terrestrial, radon, food), with the remainder from medical exposures.

12. Deterministic effects of radiation exposure occur many years after exposure and do not have a threshold dose.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
Deterministic effects, also known as tissue effects, occur rapidly (hours to weeks) after exposure to high doses of radiation and generally occur above a threshold dose.

13. Skin erythema is an example of a deterministic effect that can occur at doses of 2-5 Gy.

14. Cataracts in the lens of the eye are a stochastic effect with no clear threshold dose.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
Cataracts are a deterministic effect, occurring at doses over 2 Gy, and are cumulative [113, 112t]. [113, 112t]

15. According to the Linear No-Threshold (LNT) model, there is a safe dose of radiation below which no stochastic effects will occur.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
The LNT model, recommended by ICRP for risk calculation, implies that there is no safe dose of radiation, meaning any exposure carries some risk, however small.

16. Effective dose can be directly measured for individual patients to precisely determine their radiation risk.

Correct answer: False

17. Dose limits for specific organs, like the lens of the eye, are defined using the effective dose.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
Dose limits for particular organs (e.g., fingers, lens of the eye) are defined using the equivalent dose, whereas effective dose is used for whole-body exposures.

18. For employees over 18 years of age, the annual whole-body effective dose limit is 6 mSv.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
The annual whole-body effective dose limit for employees over 18 years is 20 mSv. The 6 mSv limit applies to trainees under 18.

19. An area is designated as a controlled area if any person working there is likely to receive an effective dose greater than 1 mSv per year.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
An area is designated as a controlled area if the effective dose is likely to exceed 6 mSv per year. An effective dose greater than 1 mSv per year indicates a supervised area.

20. The Ionising Radiation (Medical Exposure) Regulations (IR(ME)R) 2017 primarily focuses on protecting staff and the public from ionising radiation.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct answer: False
The IR(ME)R 2017 is the fundamental legislation for protecting the **patient**, based on the principles of justification and optimisation. The Ionising Radiation Regulations (IRR) 2017 deal with the protection of staff and the public.
