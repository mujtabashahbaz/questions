Chapter 2: Radiation Hazards and Protection

Test your knowledge on the fundamental principles of radiation safety and biological effects of ionising radiation as covered in Chapter 2 of Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd edition. Select True or False for each statement.

1. Ionising radiation interactions occur on the atomic scale and generate energy significantly greater than DNA covalent bond energy.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. The ionisation of a single atom generates approximately 35 eV of energy, which is significantly greater than the energy of a covalent bond in a DNA strand (around 4 eV).

2. The energy generated by the ionisation of a single atom is approximately 4 eV.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. The ionisation of a single atom generates approximately 35 eV of energy, while a covalent bond in DNA is around 4 eV.

3. Alpha particles are more likely to cause significant damage on the atomic scale compared to gamma or X-rays.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. Ionisation events (clusters of ionisation) are common when alpha particles interact with tissue, less common with beta particles, and rare with gamma or X-rays.

4. Low-LET radiation includes alpha particles.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. Alpha particles are classified as High-LET radiation, typically 10-100 keV/mm, which can damage both strands of a DNA molecule. X-rays, gamma rays, and beta particles are Low-LET radiation.

5. X-rays are likely only to damage a single DNA strand, which is more readily repaired by the body's repair mechanisms.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. The less ionising X-rays are likely only to damage a single strand, which is more readily repaired by the body's repair mechanisms.

6. The radiation weighting factor (wR) for X-rays and gamma rays is 20.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. The radiation weighting factor (wR) for X-rays and gamma rays is 1, while for alpha particles it is 20 [Table 2.1].

7. Alpha particles present a great hazard from external exposure due to their rapid energy deposition.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. Alpha particles deposit their energy very rapidly and do not penetrate very far through skin, so they do not present a great hazard from external exposure. However, if ingested or inhaled, they can cause considerable damage to internal tissues.

8. Organic polymers with high densities of hydrogen atoms, like polythene, can be used as shielding material for neutrons.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. Neutrons interact with the protons of hydrogen atoms within the tissue. Therefore, organic polymers with high densities of hydrogen atoms, such as polythene, can be used as shielding material around particle accelerators.

9. At low dose rates, tissue repair mechanisms are less effective, leading to more cell death per unit dose.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. At low dose rates, tissue repair mechanisms can repair DNA damage prior to the next exposure. More cells are killed per unit dose at high dose rates, as cumulative damage defeats these mechanisms.

10. Fractionation of radiation dose can maximize tumour killing while sparing damage to surrounding healthy tissues.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. Separating exposures by intervals of greater than 12 hours allows for tissue repair. As healthy tissues generally have more effective repair mechanisms than tumours, this can be utilised in radiotherapy.

11. The average person in the UK is exposed to an effective dose of 2.7 mSv per year from background radiation.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. The average person in the UK is exposed to an effective dose of 2.7 mSv per year from background radiation.

12. Radon gas accounts for approximately 10% of an average person's annual background exposure in the UK.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. Radon gas accounts for around 50% of an average person's annual background exposure in the UK.

13. Deterministic effects of radiation exposure occur many years after exposure, if at all.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. Deterministic effects (also known as tissue effects) occur rapidly (i.e., hours to weeks) after exposure to high doses of radiation. Stochastic effects occur many years after exposure.

14. The lowest whole-body absorbed dose likely to cause death from acute radiation sickness, in the absence of medical treatment, is around 3-5 Sv.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. In the absence of medical treatment, the lowest dose likely to cause death is around 3-5 Sv.

15. The lens of the eye is insensitive to radiation exposures.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. The lens of the eye is sensitive to radiation exposures; coagulation of proteins within the lens can occur at doses over 2 Gy, leading to cataracts and vision impairment.

16. The linear non-threshold (LNT) model implies there is no safe dose of radiation.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. The ICRP-recommended LNT model, while simple to apply, implies that there is no safe dose of radiation, which contradicts evidence of a threshold dose from atomic bomb data.

17. The risk of cancer induction from radiation exposure is highest in children under 10 years due to higher cell division rates.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True. Studies of atomic bomb survivors found that the risk of cancer induction was highest in children under 10 years at the time of exposure due to their higher cell division rate associated with growth and longer remaining lifetime.

18. Effective dose can be directly measured for individual patient exposures.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. Effective dose can only be computed; it cannot be measured directly. It is derived using mathematical modeling techniques and measurable quantities like dose-area product.

19. The Ionising Radiation Regulations (IRR) 2017 primarily deal with the protection of patients.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. The Ionising Radiation Regulations (IRR) 2017 describe legislation to minimize exposures to the public and employees. The Ionising Radiation (Medical Exposure) Regulations (IR(ME)R) 2017 is the fundamental legislation for protecting the patient.

20. The effective dose limit for employees over 18 years of age is 1 mSv per year.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False. The effective dose limit for employees over 18 years of age is 20 mSv per year. The 1 mSv per year limit applies to "Other persons" (members of the public).
